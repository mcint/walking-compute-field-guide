# Social Norms

Walking compute is not just hardware. It changes attention, gaze, availability, and the local read of whether someone is recording.

The wearer owns the ambiguity created by the rig.

## Defaults

- No surprise recording.
- No surprise transcription.
- No hidden sensors as a social default.
- If a device has a camera, microphone, or outward-facing sensor, disclose it plainly when relevant.
- If someone is not already opted into the experiment, assume they may interpret gaze-obscuring hardware as surveillance-adjacent.
- When starting a conversation with someone outside the group, prefer lifting, disabling, removing, or explaining the display.
- Do not make stealth the social default. Stealth can be an ergonomic or aesthetic experiment; it should not be a consent hack.

## Status signals

We should develop status signals that are easy for non-participants to understand.

Candidate signals:

| Signal | Meaning | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Display up / glasses lifted | Available | Most legible if hardware supports it. |
| Display down / glasses on | Computing or focused | Still ambiguous; explain when needed. |
| Visible badge: display only | No camera / no recording | Only use if actually true. |
| Projector on | Shared visual output active | Avoid projecting onto people or private surfaces. |
| Keyboard stowed | Not actively computing | Useful boundary marker. |

## Boundary situations

Use extra care in:

- First conversations with visitors.
- Conflict or mediation contexts.
- Meetings with uneven power or trust.
- Bathrooms, changing areas, sleeping areas, medical situations, or any place where cameras would obviously be creepy.
- Any context where someone asks whether they are being recorded.

## Within-group norms

People actively experimenting with the rigs should still not assume total opt-in from each other.

Recommended group defaults:

- Label whether a rig is display-only, camera-capable, microphone-capable, or recording-capable.
- Ask before recording demos or social interactions.
- Make failure and discomfort reports welcome.
- Allow “please take the glasses off for this conversation” without making it a big deal.
- Prefer visible experiments over indistinct surveillance aesthetics.

## Outside-group interaction

When interacting with people not already aware of walking compute:

1. Assume they do not know what the hardware does.
2. Assume gaze is socially meaningful even if your display obscures it.
3. Explain briefly when needed: “These are display glasses; I’m not recording.”
4. If that is not true, do not say it.
5. If the hardware can record, be explicit about whether it is recording.

## Projectors and shared display

Projectors may make the rig more socially legible by showing nearby people what is being looked at. They also introduce new problems.

Good uses:

- Demos.
- Pairing.
- Showing terminal/editor/browser state.
- Making attention visible.
- Reducing “what are they looking at?” ambiguity.

Bad uses:

- Surprise projection.
- Projecting private information.
- Projecting onto people.
- Creating glare or distraction.
- Treating shared display as automatic consent to share everything.
